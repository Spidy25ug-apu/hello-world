1Reflection on Metabase

Before diving into the reflection, let me tell you what actually metabse is. Metabase is a popular, open-source business intelligence (BI) and data visualization tool that enables users to query and analyze data without using advanced SQL. So, from the metadata, I worked on the project called pollutants of the different cities of India, where I made a graph of different cities with the maximum and minimum pollutants ID. In that graph, I have compared the two states with their cities with pollutants. At the beginning, it was difficult for me to understand how to make a graph and add different colors to it, but my group mates reached out to me, and they figured out my problem. Working on metabse is a new experience for me. I was a little bit kind of excited during the project because I wanted to learn new things about it, and I thought that maybe this kind of work would help me in the future. Doing this work with group mates is amazing, joyful, and thoughtful, and we developed a learning environment among us. I hope that this will be more useful in making the different projects in the coming academic year.

In this unit, I learned what failure actually is and how the great man deals with it. Before this, I saw failure as something else, like shame, to avoid, hide, or feel embarrassed about. I believed that failure meant not being good enough. However, after reading this chapter, my perspective on failure has completely changed.

One concept that stuck in my mind was “intelligent failure”. This means failing to clearly define purpose, proper research, and a meaningful goal. It is not about making careless mistakes, but about taking thought risks. When we fail in this way, we get only valuable insights that help us improve.

Reflection on Unit 2 

In Unit 2, I studied the BRIS, where I explored the wide range of topics that helped me to understand how mathematics and computers are connected. At the beginning, it seemed technical, but gradually they became meaningful as I practiced on them. I have learned about the number systems, that is, binary, decimal, and hexadecimal conversions. Initially, it was difficult and confusing, but with practice, it was easy. I got to know how computers actually perform calculations at a fundamental level. Another topic that grabbed my attention was fun with images. I know what an image is, but not in detail, not more. learning that images are made up of pixels, each pixel holds a value, was fascinating. It changed my mind about how I looked at digital images. I learned how image quality depends on image resolution. It was challenging for me to understand how flipping or resizing happens based on logical operations. Moving ahead, I knew that optimization and formal analysis helped me think logically, instead of solving problems directly. These topics I found difficult and confusing. The Bridges of Konigsberg problem was good but not simple. It showed how real-world puzzles led to development of graph theory. Finally, learning about abstraction and modeling taught me how to simplify complex problems. Overall, I say that this kind of learning improved my problem-solving skills at some point.
